# Abstract

Autonomous trading agents are software systems that perceive the state of a financial market, reason over heterogeneous numerical and textual signals, and autonomously execute orders in pursuit of return and risk objectives. Building such agents is hard because markets are fast, partially observable, non-stationary, and adversarial: prices are generated by many strategic participants whose collective behaviour invalidates the i.i.d. assumptions underlying most supervised learning. Earlier responses to these difficulties — manual discretionary trading, rule-based algorithmic execution, and standard supervised machine learning on tabular price data — each captured part of the problem but left structural gaps in adaptation, in the integration of unstructured information (news, filings, social media), and in closing the loop between prediction and execution under transaction costs. This survey synthesises the now-rapid convergence of two research programs that aim to close that loop: (i) deep reinforcement learning (DRL) for quantitative trading, exemplified by the FinRL family of open-source libraries (Liu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022) and surveyed by Hambly, Xu and Yang (2023); and (ii) large language model (LLM) agents for financial decision-making, including FinMem (Yu et al., 2024), FinAgent (Zhang et al., 2024), TradingGPT (Li et al., 2023), FinCon (Yu et al., 2024),  QuantAgent (Wang et al., 2024) and TradingAgents (Xiao et al., 2024). We adopt the classical definition of an agent due to Wooldridge and Jennings (1995)  and Russell and Norvig, formalise an autonomous trading agent as a closed perception–reasoning–action loop, and present a nine-step abstract workflow. We then catalogue the supporting ecosystem — market simulators such as ABIDES (Byrd, Hybinette & Balch, 2020), orchestration frameworks such as LangChain, AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023)  and CrewAI, and the LLM-agent survey of Ding et al. (2024) — and discuss the regulatory landscape emerging around AI-driven trading (FSB, 2024; IOSCO, 2025). The survey is written for computer scientists, AI researchers, and data scientists without a trading background; financial terms are defined on first use, and reported performance figures are presented critically as backtest results rather than as evidence of live profitability.

# Overview / Introduction

## 1. Intuitive Problem Framing

Financial markets are among the most demanding decision-making environments in applied artificial intelligence. They are *fast*: in modern equity markets, quotes update at millisecond resolution and orders propagate through the exchange’s matching engine — for example, the NASDAQ ITCH and OUCH protocols modelled by ABIDES (Byrd, Hybinette & Balch, 2020)  — in microseconds. They are *noisy*: financial time series exhibit an exceptionally low signal-to-noise ratio (Liu et al., 2022, arXiv:2211.03107), which is one of three factors that Liu et al. explicitly name (alongside survivorship bias and backtest overfitting) as making finance “a particularly difficult playground for deep reinforcement learning”.   They are *partially observable*: an agent sees prices and a subset of the limit-order book (LOB), but never the latent intentions of other traders. They are *sequential and path-dependent*: today’s order changes tomorrow’s price through *market impact*, the mechanism formalised in the optimal-execution framework of Almgren and Chriss (2001).  They are *multi-agent*: every learning algorithm shares the venue with high-frequency market makers, institutional execution algorithms, retail flow, and increasingly other autonomous agents. Finally, the relevant information is simultaneously *quantitative* (prices, volumes, fundamentals) and *textual* (news, earnings transcripts, social media), motivating architectures that can ingest both modalities (Zhang et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024).

## 2. Why Traditional Approaches Were Not Enough

The history of automation in trading is a sequence of partial fixes to this hard problem. *Manual discretionary trading* — a human reading the tape — does not scale to thousands of instruments and cannot react at machine speed. *Rule-based algorithmic trading* (e.g., VWAP, TWAP, momentum and mean-reversion rule families, and 1980s-era portfolio-insurance and index-arbitrage programs that the SEC found accounted for nearly 60% of NYSE volume on Black Monday, 19 October 1987,  when the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 508 points or 22.6%)  is fast and auditable but, as Zhang et al. (2024) note in motivating FinAgent, “rule-based trading systems are rigid and struggle to adapt to market volatility, often resulting in underperformance in evolving markets”.  *Standard supervised machine learning* on tabular features — predicting next-day returns with gradient-boosted trees or LSTMs — improves pattern recognition but treats trading as a one-shot prediction problem rather than as sequential decision-making under transaction costs; it cannot, on its own, decide *when* to act, *how much* to trade, or *how* to manage inventory. *Deep reinforcement learning* directly optimises a policy over the full state–action loop and was a major advance (Hambly, Xu & Yang, 2023; Pricope, 2021), but pure DRL agents lack a natural mechanism for interpreting unstructured text, are sample-inefficient, and often overfit historical regimes (Liu et al., 2022). These limitations motivated the current generation of LLM-based agents, which combine reasoning over text with planning, memory, and tool use.

## 3. Definition and Purpose

Following Wooldridge and Jennings (1995), an *agent* is a computer system situated in some environment that is *autonomous*, *reactive*, *pro-active*, and *socially capable*. Russell and Norvig’s textbook definition (widely cited; *Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach*) treats an agent as anything that perceives its environment through sensors and acts upon it through actuators.  Specialising these definitions to finance, we define an **autonomous trading agent** as a software system that (i) continuously perceives a market environment through structured (price, order-book, fundamentals) and unstructured (news, filings, social media) inputs; (ii) maintains internal state, including memory of past observations and decisions; (iii) reasons about expected return, risk, and constraints; and (iv) emits orders to a real or simulated exchange, with the goal of optimising a long-horizon objective such as cumulative return, Sharpe ratio (the ratio of mean excess return to its standard deviation), or drawdown-adjusted utility. Crucially, an autonomous trading agent is *not* merely a predictive model: it is a closed decision-making loop in which actions affect future observations through market impact and inventory.

## 4. Historical Development

The lineage runs from *manual* floor trading through *rule-based* program trading in the 1980s, to the *quantitative/statistical* era (factor models, statistical arbitrage) catalysed by academic work on optimal execution such as Almgren and Chriss (2001). The 2010s brought *machine learning* trading — supervised learning on engineered features and, later, deep learning on raw price tensors. From around 2018 the field pivoted to *deep reinforcement learning*: Liu et al. (2018, arXiv:1811.07522) demonstrated a practical DRL approach for stock trading, and the FinRL library (Liu et al., 2021, arXiv:2011.09607; ACM ICAIF version 2021, arXiv:2111.09395) and its successor FinRL-Meta (Liu et al., 2022, NeurIPS Datasets & Benchmarks)  established reproducible benchmarks. The most recent wave, made tractable by the launch of OpenAI’s ChatGPT (GPT-3.5) on 30 November 2022 and the multimodal GPT-4 on 14 March 2023, applies *LLM-based agents* — TradingGPT (Li et al., 2023), FinMem (Yu et al., 2024), FinAgent (Zhang et al., 2024), QuantAgent (Wang et al., 2024), FinCon (Yu et al., 2024) and TradingAgents (Xiao et al., 2024) — to integrate textual reasoning, memory, and multi-agent coordination on top of the underlying DRL and execution machinery.

## 5. High-Level Abstract Workflow

Modern autonomous trading agents instantiate, in various combinations, the following nine-step pipeline:

1. **Observe.** Subscribe to live or replayed feeds of prices, order-book snapshots, news, filings, and social media.
1. **Collect and normalise.** Align time stamps, handle missing data, and adjust for corporate actions; FinRL-Meta packages this as a DataOps layer over gym-style environments (Liu et al., 2022). 
1. **Extract signals.** Compute technical indicators, fundamental ratios, sentiment scores, and (in multimodal agents such as FinAgent) chart embeddings from K-line images (Zhang et al., 2024). 
1. **Form hypotheses.** Use a reasoning module — historically a DRL policy network, increasingly an LLM with chain-of-thought prompting — to combine signals into an explicit trading thesis.
1. **Decide.** Map the thesis to a target position (buy/sell/hold and size), conditioned on the agent’s *profile* (risk preference, horizon) — a design pattern made explicit in FinMem’s Profiling module (Yu et al., 2024). 
1. **Check risk.** Apply position limits, drawdown caps, and concentration constraints; in FinCon (Yu et al., 2024), a dedicated risk-control component initiates self-critique between episodes. 
1. **Execute.** Schedule child orders to minimise market impact, drawing on optimal-execution theory (Almgren & Chriss, 2001) and, in simulation, on multi-agent venues such as ABIDES (Byrd et al., 2020).
1. **Monitor.** Track realised profit-and-loss (P&L), slippage (difference between expected and realised execution price), and compliance metrics; raise human review when behaviour drifts.
1. **Learn.** Update parameters (DRL gradient steps) or memories and reflections (LLM agents) to incorporate new outcomes; FinCon’s *conceptual verbal reinforcement* propagates lessons selectively across agents  (Yu et al., 2024, NeurIPS).

## 6. How Modern LLM Agents Extend Earlier Systems

LLM agents add four capabilities that pure DRL trading systems lack. First, **textual reasoning over unstructured data**: news headlines, earnings transcripts, and analyst reports are first-class inputs rather than features distilled offline. Second, **structured memory**: FinMem and TradingGPT (Li et al., 2023) explicitly emulate human short-, medium-, and long-term memory layers,  with recency-, relevance-, and importance-weighted retrieval. Third, **tool use**: agents call external APIs for data retrieval, charting, or quantitative computation, mirroring the broader pattern formalised in AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023) and LangChain. Fourth, **multi-agent coordination**: TradingAgents (Xiao et al., 2024) and FinCon (Yu et al., 2024) instantiate trading-firm hierarchies — fundamental, sentiment, technical, and news analysts; bull/bear researchers; traders; and risk managers — that debate, reflect, and aggregate views.  Ding et al. (2024) survey this design space and classify finance LLM agents into news-driven, reflection-driven, debate-driven, and RL-driven families. 

These capabilities come with new risks. LLMs **hallucinate** facts (as discussed in Xiao et al., 2024, citing Ji et al., 2023). They are **overconfident** about out-of-distribution events. They are vulnerable to **data leakage** when training-cutoff dates overlap with backtest periods — a particularly insidious issue because LLM evaluation periods in published trading papers (e.g., 2020–2023 for the S&P 500 evaluation reported in QuantAgent; January–March 2024 for TradingAgents)  often sit entirely *before* the paper’s release, making it difficult to rule out that the underlying base model has memorised the news it is asked to “react” to. Reported cumulative returns and Sharpe ratios should therefore be read as backtest results in idealised execution settings, not as evidence of live profitability.

It is important to note that several of the most-cited LLM trading systems remain **arXiv preprints without peer review** at the time of writing — notably TradingGPT (Li et al., 2023), QuantAgent (Wang et al., 2024), and TradingAgents (Xiao et al., 2024) — while others (FinMem, FinAgent, FinCon, FinRL-Meta) have appeared in peer-reviewed venues (AAAI Spring Symposium, ACM KDD, NeurIPS).

Regulators have begun to track these developments. The Financial Stability Board’s November 2024 report *The Financial Stability Implications of Artificial Intelligence* identifies four AI-related vulnerabilities that “stand out for their potential to increase systemic risk … (i) third-party dependencies and service provider concentration; (ii) market correlations; (iii) cyber risks; and (iv) model risk, data quality and governance,”  warning that “widespread use of common AI models and data sources could lead to increased correlations in trading, lending, and pricing”  and so “amplify market stress”  (FSB, 2024). IOSCO’s consultation report *Artificial Intelligence in Capital Markets: Use Cases, Risks, and Challenges* (CR/01/2025), published 12 March 2025, extends this analysis to algorithmic trading, investment research, and sentiment analysis. U.S. regulators including the SEC and CFTC have raised parallel concerns about explainability, market manipulation (spoofing, layering),  and “AI-washing”  (Johnson, 2024, CFTC speech).

# Concise Bullet-Point Overview

## What Autonomous Trading Agents Are

- Software systems that perceive markets, reason about state, and place orders without continuous human intervention.
- Defined as closed decision loops, not one-shot predictors (Wooldridge & Jennings, 1995; Russell & Norvig).
- Modern instantiations combine deep reinforcement learning with large language model reasoning.

## Why They Emerged

- Markets are too fast and too data-rich for purely manual decision-making.
- Rule-based and supervised-ML systems cannot adapt to non-stationary, multi-agent dynamics.
- The rise of capable LLMs (ChatGPT, Nov 2022; GPT-4, Mar 2023) made textual reasoning over news and filings tractable.

## What Problems They Solve

- Integrating numerical and textual signals in one decision pipeline (Zhang et al., 2024).
- Sequential decision-making under transaction costs and inventory constraints (Almgren & Chriss, 2001; Hambly et al., 2023).
- Codifying trading-firm workflows (analyst → researcher → trader → risk manager) as multi-agent systems (Xiao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024).

## Limitations of Older Methods

- Manual trading: does not scale.
- Rule-based algorithms: rigid, brittle in regime change.
- Supervised ML: ignores action–consequence loop and execution costs.
- Pure DRL: sample-inefficient, opaque, struggles with text.

## High-Level Workflow

- Observe → collect/normalise → extract signals → form hypotheses → decide → check risk → execute → monitor → learn (nine-step loop).

## How LLMs Changed the Field

- First-class textual reasoning over news, filings, and social media.
- Layered, human-like memory (FinMem, TradingGPT).
- Tool use and external API calls (AutoGen, LangChain).
- Multi-agent debate and coordination (TradingAgents, FinCon).
- Multimodal inputs including K-line chart images (FinAgent).

## Supporting Frameworks and Tools

- DRL libraries: FinRL, FinRL-Meta.
- Market simulators: ABIDES, ABIDES-Gym.
- Orchestration: LangChain, AutoGen, CrewAI.
- Memory: vector stores (Chroma, FAISS, Pinecone).
- Surveys: Hambly, Xu & Yang (2023) on DRL; Ding et al. (2024) on LLM agents.

## Key Risks and Open Problems

- Hallucination and overconfidence in LLM outputs.
- Data leakage: LLM training cutoffs overlap with backtest windows.
- Backtest overfitting and survivorship bias (Liu et al., 2022).
- Market-impact and transaction-cost realism — most papers under-model these.
- Systemic risks from correlated AI behaviour: FSB (2024) names “market correlations” among four top vulnerabilities; IOSCO (2025) consultation flags herding and “collusive” behaviours.
- Regulatory uncertainty around explainability, audit trails, and market manipulation (Johnson, 2024).

# Research Checklist

- [ ] Is each framework genuinely open-source, and under what licence (MIT, BSD, Apache, proprietary)?
- [ ] Are reported returns from out-of-sample backtests, paper trading, or live trading?
- [ ] Were realistic transaction costs, slippage, and market impact modelled (e.g., via Almgren–Chriss), or assumed zero?
- [ ] Were results validated on data strictly *after* the LLM’s training cutoff to rule out leakage?
- [ ] Are baselines (Buy-and-Hold, MACD, mean-reversion, DRL) implemented with comparable hyperparameter tuning, or are they straw men?
- [ ] Does the framework support real broker execution, simulation only (e.g., ABIDES), or replay of historical tape?
- [ ] Are Sharpe ratio and maximum drawdown reported with confidence intervals or only as point estimates?
- [ ] Were results averaged over multiple random seeds and multiple market regimes (bull, bear, sideways, crash)?
- [ ] For LLM agents: was the same LLM version and temperature used across all comparisons?
- [ ] Are the data sources (news, filings, social media) reproducible, or behind paywalls?
- [ ] Does the system include a risk-management layer with position and drawdown limits?
- [ ] Is the paper peer-reviewed (and where), or only an arXiv preprint? (Verified peer-reviewed: FinRL-Meta, FinMem, FinAgent, FinCon, ABIDES. Preprint-only at time of writing: TradingGPT, QuantAgent, TradingAgents, Ding et al. survey.)
- [ ] Does the paper disclose conflicts of interest with trading firms or vendors?
- [ ] Has the regulatory exposure been considered (FSB 2024 systemic-risk categories; IOSCO CR/01/2025; FINRA Rule 3110 human oversight;  SEC AI-washing enforcement)?
